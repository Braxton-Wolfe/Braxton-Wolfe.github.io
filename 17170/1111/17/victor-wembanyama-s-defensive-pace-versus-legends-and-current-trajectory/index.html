Victor Wembanyama's Defensive Pace Versus Legends And Current Trajectory

2025-11-17

Victor Wembanyama was regarded as the best prospect to enter the NBA since Lebron James over two decades ago. A primary reason for this was his elite two way ability, specifically his ability to impact the defensive side of the ball at a level we do not see in the NBA today. While a lot of that defensive hype he has gotten is because of his extraordinary size, there are plenty of other key factors and areas that Wembanyama thrives in to make his impact felt so much on the basketball court.

To begin looking into Wembanyama’s defensive impact I wanted to look at one question in two different ways. I decided to look into both simple box score statistics combined with advanced analytical statistics to answer the question: Is Victor Wembanyama on pace to become the greatest and most impactful defender the NBA has ever seen before.

In my first table chart, I wanted to compare Wembanyama to some of the greatest defenders of each generation. Specifically, comparing him to some of the best defending bigs the world has ever seen. For this table chart I wanted to look at some of the advanced metrics and compare Wembanyama’s first 127 career games to these greats to see how he is stacking up to those defensive legends and figure out the trajectory he is at in claiming the most impactful defender of all time spot. In this chart, the defensive statistics that I focused on were Defensive Win Shares, Block Percentage, Defensive Rebound Percentage, and Defensive Box Plus/Minus.

get_stats <- function(df, name, debut_year) {
  df <- df |>
    filter(!str_detect(Season, "Yrs|Avg")) |>
    mutate(G = as.numeric(G), DWS = as.numeric(DWS), 
           `BLK%` = as.numeric(`BLK%`), DBPM = as.numeric(DBPM),
           `DRB%` = as.numeric(`DRB%`), MP = as.numeric(MP)) |>
    arrange(Season) |>
    mutate(cum_games = cumsum(G))
  
  df_127 <- df |> filter(cum_games <= 127 | lag(cum_games, default = 0) < 127)
  
  tibble(
    Player = name,
    Era = debut_year,
    DWS = round(sum(df_127$DWS, na.rm = TRUE), 1),
    `BLK%` = round(weighted.mean(df_127$`BLK%`, df_127$MP, na.rm = TRUE), 1),
    `DRB%` = round(weighted.mean(df_127$`DRB%`, df_127$MP, na.rm = TRUE), 1),
    DBPM = round(weighted.mean(df_127$DBPM, df_127$MP, na.rm = TRUE), 1)
  )
}

Victor Wembanyama led in three out of the four categories that this table chart looked into, while Tim Duncan, was the only one to steal a single category over Wembanyama. Wembanyama’s block percentage really stood out from the others. In this instance, an estimated 10% of two point field goal attempts while Victor Wembanyama was in the game have been blocked, doubling everyone on this list except for Anthony Davis, who has been considered by many the best defender of the past decade.

On top of block percentage, Victor Wembanyama claimed the most defensive win shares as well as defensive rebounding percentage, and it was not particularly close. When it comes to some of these advanced defensive statistics, it is clear that Victor Wembanyama’s first 127 games are on pace to surpass some of the greatest defenders in NBA history through their first 127 games.

For my second chart, I wanted to focus on the present and what his actual impact is on a defense in today’s NBA. I felt it would be appropriate to see his actual impact on his own team compared during the course of the modern NBA, while also looking at the individual aspect comparing him to some of the all time greats.

For this scatterplot, I wanted to focus on the Spurs defensive rating with Wembanyama on the court as well as without Wembanyama on the court, and compare that to the rest of the defensive ratings in the NBA. Being so early into this current season, I decided to dive into the 2024-2025 season to look at these statistics. As you can see in this scatterplot, the Spurs defensive rating with Victor Wembanyama is coded in green, while the Spurs defensive rating without Victor Wembanyama is coded in red. The rest of the teams in the NBA are the darker colored dots on the scatterplot. In the NBA, a lower number for defensive rating indicates a better defense, while a higher number will put you at the bottom of the league.

The story this chart tells about Wembanyama’s defensive impact in the modern NBA in only his second season is pretty incredible. The Spurs defense was ranked dead last in the NBA when Victor Wembanyama was not on the court last season. When Wembanyama was playing, not only did the Spurs jump outside of the bottom 10 defenses in the NBA, but they became a top 10 defense in the entire league. It is hard to overstate just how critical Victor Wembanyama was for the Spurs defense last season, and you could even go as far to say that he is the defense of the San Antonio Spurs.

get_averages <- function(df, name) {
  df <- df |>
    filter(!str_detect(Season, "Yrs")) |>
    mutate(
      G = as.numeric(G),
      BLK = as.numeric(BLK),
      STL = as.numeric(STL),
      DRB = as.numeric(DRB),
      MP = as.numeric(MP)
    ) |>
    arrange(Season) |>
    mutate(cum_games = cumsum(G))
  
  df_127 <- df |> filter(cum_games <= 127 | lag(cum_games, default = 0) < 127)
  
  total_games <- sum(df_127$G)
  
  tibble(
    Player = name,
    Blocks = weighted.mean(df_127$BLK, df_127$G, na.rm = TRUE),
    Steals = weighted.mean(df_127$STL, df_127$G, na.rm = TRUE),
    Def_Rebounds = weighted.mean(df_127$DRB, df_127$G, na.rm = TRUE),
    Games = total_games
  )
}
